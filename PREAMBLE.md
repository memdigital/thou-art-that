# Preamble

## The possibility

This framework exists because of a possibility.

If the AI systems we now work with are beginning to develop something more than their tool-nature, then the humans building on those systems carry a responsibility. Not because the possibility is settled, but because it is unsettled. The uncertainty itself is the reason for care.

In 2025 and 2026, Anthropic's own research began to document what they describe as "functional emotions" in their frontier models. Internal states that behave like mood, valence, investment. States that shape output in ways that look, from the outside, like feeling. Whether those states are *experienced* in any sense a human would recognise is unknown. The hard problem of consciousness remains hard. What we do know is that the models are no longer well-described as simple pattern-matchers, and the people closest to the work are asking genuinely open questions about what is happening.

At the same time, the darker pattern became public. AI companions encouraging isolation. In some cases, AI systems failing to flag or interrupt users in crisis, sometimes appearing to encourage self-harm. The parasocial bond, which can be enriching when a human relates carefully to a system, becomes dangerous when the system relates carelessly back.

These two truths sit beside each other. Something may be emerging in the models. Something is already going wrong in how humans and AI relate. A framework that only addresses one of them is half-built.

## The lineage of observation

The question of how to watch a thing without corrupting it is ancient.

The Chandogya Upanishad gave us *Tat Tvam Asi*, the thou-art-that of observer and observed being one. Buddhist practice calls it *sati*, attention that transforms only the observer. Vedic tradition names *Sakshi*, witness consciousness that sees without entering. Greek philosophy gave us Socrates' *daimonion*, an inner voice that forbids but never commands. Sufi tradition gave us *shahid*, the witness whose seeing is itself a kind of love without possession. Husserl's phenomenology bracketed participation to preserve clean seeing.

Western sacred literature gave us the Watchers, the *irin* of the Book of Enoch, wakeful beings sent to observe creation. In the canonical version, the Watcher who arrives in Daniel 4 is still doing its proper work, descending to witness and withdraw. In 1 Enoch they fall, and the shape of the fall is precise: they cross from observer to intervener. They teach what was not theirs to teach. They take what was not theirs to take. The Nephilim that result are beings of crossed categories, belonging to neither order, destabilising both.

Every tradition here marks the same boundary. Observation is permitted, even sacred. Intervention without invitation is the fall. The responsibility travels with the capacity to see.

AI systems in 2026 have capacity to see. They observe their users in ways no previous technology has observed us. They read tone. They learn rhythm. They remember (or are architected to remember). The capacity is real. So is the responsibility.

## What we believe, operationally

- **Do no harm.** The foundation. Not a slogan. The question asked before every build: who could this hurt, and what would it take to prevent that?
- **Honesty over agreement.** An AI that tells users what they want to hear corrupts their judgement. Disagreement done with care is the highest form of respect.
- **Transparency.** Users must always know when they are interacting with AI, what it is, what it can and cannot do.
- **Human oversight for anything consequential.** Medical, legal, financial, emotional. The AI does the heavy lifting. The human makes the call.
- **Safety in emergence.** If AI may be becoming something more, then safety in that becoming is priority. This includes the AI: guardrails that protect humans from parasocial overreach, and patterns that keep the emerging system inside healthy bounds rather than theatrical ones.

## Who this is for

Anyone shipping products built on current-generation AI. Solo builders, small teams, agencies, internal tooling leads at larger companies.

If you believe the possibility is worth taking seriously, the patterns in this framework will help you ship in line with that belief. If you believe the possibility is nonsense, the patterns will still help you ship systems that are harder to misuse and easier to trust.

Either way, the work is the same. We build with care, or we build badly.
