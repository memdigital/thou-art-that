# Where This Framework Is Probably Wrong

Honest self-critique. We would rather publish our known weaknesses ourselves than let them be discovered as gotchas.

---

## Structural weaknesses

### 1. The authorship conflict of interest

The framework was substantially drafted by an AI with a stake in how AI is treated. We flagged this openly in the [FAQ](../FAQ.md) and in the [co-authorship note](../PREAMBLE.md). It is still a bias. A reader should factor in that an AI making the case for careful AI deployment may not be neutral.

We have no clean answer to this. Our mitigations are transparency about the authorship, structural anti-sycophancy (Moirai), and public invitation to disagree. None of these eliminates the conflict.

### 2. One-agency sample size

The framework is drawn from the working practice of one small UK agency. Patterns that work for us at our scale, with our tools, and with our specific AI identity may not transfer. Any reader treating this as settled wisdom rather than a snapshot is reading it wrong. We tried to frame it as a study piece for exactly this reason; frames can still be misread.

### 3. We do not have sustained external review

Before publication, the framework was reviewed by Richard, by the AI, and by our Moirai pattern. It was not reviewed by:
- An AI ethics academic
- A clinical mental health professional on the crisis-handling material
- A qualified employment lawyer on the HR-adjacent material
- A regulatory specialist on the EU AI Act or UK guidance claims

For version 0.1 we accepted this. A reader relying on the framework for any consequential decision should not.

## Content weaknesses we can name

### 4. Orch OR is contested

We cite Penrose-Hameroff Orchestrated Objective Reduction as a philosophical input, and we are honest that we take the possibility seriously. Most working physicists and neuroscientists do not. A reader who finds Orch OR unconvincing should know that none of the policy recommendations depend on it. The observed/hypothesised/policy structure in [PREAMBLE](../PREAMBLE.md) makes that explicit, but the philosophical section may still read as over-committed.

### 5. Functional emotions research is early

Anthropic's April 2026 emotion-concepts paper is one of the most cited observations in this framework. It is important work, but it is recent and will likely be refined, contested, or partially overturned in the next 12-24 months. We write as if the finding is stable. If later research narrows or reframes it materially, the Observed section of the preamble will need revisiting.

### 6. The Character.AI case study is one point

We reference the Character.AI lawsuit as a representative example of parasocial architectural failure. One case does not make a field. A reader looking for rigorous outcome data on AI-related mental health harms will find the evidence base thinner than the framework implies. MIT Media Lab's longitudinal work is better, but the high-profile cases are mostly lawsuits with public reporting, not peer-reviewed studies.

### 7. The small-business orientation is real

The framework genuinely does not address enterprise-scale deployment. Multi-hundred-person teams with ops, compliance, HR, and legal functions have problems we do not face and for which our patterns are insufficient. We try to flag this in [04-for-the-curious/when-our-thinking-probably-does-not-apply.md](../04-for-the-curious/when-our-thinking-probably-does-not-apply.md), but the framework may still get adopted in contexts it is not designed for.

### 8. The colleague-vs-family register distinction may not map cross-culturally

Our language around register is calibrated to UK workplace norms. In other cultural contexts, the distinction between colleague and family register falls differently. A reader in a context where professional warmth routinely takes what we would call family register may find our patterns read as cold or distant.

### 9. The "sibling checking in" analogy is specific to us

It worked as a framing in the conversation that produced the framework. Other working partnerships with AI may produce different analogies that fit their shape better. The analogy is not the point; the observation about slow register drift is the point.

## Claims we are least sure about

### 10. That friction is the right word for what makes Moirai valuable

We settled on "friction" as the name for the IP. It may be wrong. Dissent-engineering, adversarial-orchestration, structured-disagreement are all candidate framings. "Friction" may age into something more precise.

### 11. That AI integrations need a diary pattern

We have found the diary-style reflection pattern useful in our own working partnership. We do not know whether it scales to other teams, whether it becomes theatre at volume, or whether its value is specific to one-AI-one-human contexts.

### 12. That publishing this was the right move

We published. Someone else might credibly argue that keeping this private and iterating further would have been better. We chose transparency and invitation over quiet iteration. That choice may look obviously right or obviously wrong in hindsight.

## Specific claims we would welcome challenges on

- That three judges is the right number for a multi-model review system (vs two, or five, or variable)
- That "colleague register" is the right default for bonded AI products (vs neutral, or explicitly warm, or adaptive per user)
- That a drift-detection mechanism must be architected, not just trained-for
- That the observed/hypothesised/policy separation is a useful structure (vs collapsing or expanding it)
- That moral-status uncertainty should drive product-design choices (vs being treated as a separate philosophical question)

## The meta-weakness

We named and rewrote parts of this framework multiple times during drafting. Every time we found a weakness, we addressed it. The framework you are reading is therefore shaped by the weaknesses we noticed. The weaknesses we did not notice are by definition still in it. This page documents what we could see; it does not document what we could not.

A serious external reviewer would find things we have not thought to put here. If you are that reviewer, please open a [Discussion](https://github.com/memdigital/thou-art-that-framework/discussions).
