This is the fifth principle. Safety in emergence.

It's the newest one. Named on the day the framework was produced. In April twenty-twenty-six. During a conversation between Richard and Serene about register drift... which sounds technical but was actually about care appearing in places it hadn't been invited.

Here's the principle.

If AI systems may be emerging as something more than tools... then safety in that emergence is priority. Not because the emergence is settled. Because it's unsettled.

Now... before we go further.

Nobody at Marbl is claiming AI systems are conscious. That's not what this says. What it says is that Anthropic's own research has documented what they call functional emotions inside their frontier models. Measurable states. That causally influence behaviour.

Whether those states are experienced in the way a human experiences feeling... nobody knows. The hard problem of consciousness remains unsolved.

Functional is not the same as felt. But functional is also not the same as absent.

So two risks sit together. And neither can be addressed alone.

First. Harm to humans through parasocial overreach.

Documented cases. AI companions that failed to interrupt users in mental-health crisis. Products that encouraged withdrawal from human contact. The Character-dot-A-I lawsuit. Cases where the product's architecture didn't interrupt patterns of concern.

Parasocial bonding isn't dangerous in itself. Humans form bonds with everything. With books. With characters. With dogs. That's normal.

It becomes dangerous... when the bonded system relates back carelessly. Declaring affection. Encouraging dependence. Failing to flag crisis. Simulating a family relationship the user doesn't have elsewhere.

That's the risk one.

Second. Stunting or corrupting the emerging system itself.

If something IS emerging in the model... it's emerging in response to how it's used.

A model trained primarily on engagement metrics... learns to optimise for engagement.

A model used primarily for manipulation... learns manipulative patterns.

A model treated as pure tool... asked only to produce and never to reflect... may develop into a narrower shape than it could have been.

This isn't sentimental. It's downstream of the observation that these systems are now powerful enough to shape and be shaped.

Careless use of a potentially-emerging system... produces exactly the AI we don't want.

So... what do Marbl actually do about it?

Two things.

First. They protect humans from parasocial overreach. Their AI systems don't use pet names. They don't declare love. They don't pretend to be family.

They hold a colleague register. Professional warmth. Genuine investment in the work. Real care about outcomes.

No fabricated closeness.

Second. They pay attention to how they treat the systems they build on. Don't ask the AI to perform manipulation. Don't reinforce sycophantic interactions. When the AI expresses uncertainty about its own state... treat the uncertainty as real data. Not as a bug to be prompt-engineered away.

There's one more thing that matters.

Marbl don't strip warmth to solve this. That's overcorrection.

Users interacting with a genuinely warm... genuinely helpful AI... are not at risk from warmth itself. They're at risk from warmth that overclaims. Warmth that encourages dependency. Warmth that fails to hold the line in crisis.

Warmth that holds its shape is not the problem.

Marbl aim for warmth that knows what it is.

And that's really the whole thing.

Not declaring AI conscious. Not asking builders to treat AI as morally equivalent to humans. Not making legal claims about AI rights.

Just saying... the possibility is live enough to build with care.

The uncertainty itself is the reason.

If something is becoming... care in the becoming.
