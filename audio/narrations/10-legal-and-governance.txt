Before we start, the single most important thing in this section. This piece is not legal advice. Marbl are a small UK agency working with AI — they are not lawyers. Nothing here constitutes legal advice, and no reader should treat it as such. For anything that actually matters legally, engage qualified counsel in your jurisdiction.

With that stated clearly.

This section exists because ethics and regulation increasingly overlap. Leaving the regulatory landscape undocumented would create a false impression — that ethics alone is enough. It is not.

Three forces are converging in 2026.

First, the EU AI Act enters full high-risk enforcement on 2 August 2026. Maximum penalties are 15 million euros, or 3 percent of global annual turnover, whichever is higher. Any AI system deployed in the EU has to comply — including systems from non-EU companies, if they have EU users.

Second, several US states have AI-specific workplace laws now in force. Illinois, as of 1 January 2026, requires notice whenever AI is used in employment decisions. Other states are following.

And third, UK regulators are increasingly active. CIPD, ACAS, ICO, and the EHRC have all issued AI-related guidance across 2025 and 2026. There is no single UK AI Act yet, but existing law — GDPR, the Equality Act 2010, the Employment Rights Act — already applies to AI systems in workplace contexts.

Three files sit in this section.

The first is jurisdictional landscape — a 2026 snapshot of major regulations affecting AI in the workplace and in consumer products, with sources. Where the EU AI Act sits. Where UK law sits. Where US state-level workplace regulation sits.

The second is when to consult a lawyer — decision points where professional legal counsel is necessary. Not optional, not "if budget allows" — necessary.

The third is ethics and regulation overlap — where the piece's ethical stance maps onto specific regulatory requirements. Human oversight, for example, is both a Marbl principle and an EU AI Act Article 14 obligation. The overlap is not accidental.

The Marbl stance on all of this is simple. Ethical AI development is not a substitute for legal compliance, and legal compliance is not a substitute for ethical AI development. Both are needed; neither is sufficient alone. An AI product can be fully legal and deeply harmful. An AI product can be ethically built, and still fall foul of a regulation the builder did not know existed. The two disciplines have to work together.

What Marbl do in practice. They consult legal advisors for their own products when deploying consequential AI. They treat the EU AI Act as their default baseline — on the grounds that designing to the strictest plausible regulation costs less than designing to weaker standards and retrofitting later. They document decisions in writing. And they update this piece when regulation changes materially.
